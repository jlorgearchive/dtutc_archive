A Brief History of the DTUTC


Since the conception of Atomism in Ancient Greece, the theory of multiple universes co-existing together has generated fascinating discussion and imagination. Fast forwarding to the 20th century, that idea has been proven correct by our nation's brightest minds.

In the summer of 1953, Doctor Frederick Thompson from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology proposed the Thompson's Equation, an equation that, if applied correctly, can be used to rip open the space-time fabric that separates our universe from other universes. Dr. Thompson graciously submitted his works to the Department of Defense, and finally to President Eisenhower. President Eisenhower then promptly created the Office of Transuniversal Communication on July 5, 1953 with Dr. Thompson as its first and only Director. The Office then became an Executive Department through Congress five months later.

The Department was small, but efficient. In just two years, we managed to establish a direct sub-atomic connection to another world. New projects and experiments continued to be created, and through time, better and stronger connection were established. From just observing a small amount of quarks moving around, we now have the ability to send and recieve large amounts of information from Planet Earth in so many universes that are so different from us.

A new breakthrough happened in February of 1962 when Professor Dimitri Bonnet from the University of California, who was also a long-time employee at the Department, discovered the Bonnet Tunnel. A modification to the original Thompson's Equation that can be used to connect to different temporal periods within a five-thousand-year range. This discovery opened up infinite possibilities for the Department. Not only can we get information from another Earth, we can now get information from that Earth's future, something that those Earthlings might not even have the ability to do. It was also in 1962 that our Department was given the now-current name of the Department of Trans-Universal and Temporal Communication.

The Department has, is and will contribute priceless knowledge in the fields of science, technology, and history for the security of our nation.
